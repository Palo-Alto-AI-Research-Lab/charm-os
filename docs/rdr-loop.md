# The RDR Loop

RDR is the decision method at the center of the framework. It exists because **recall alone is not enough** for any non-trivial decision, and **research without recall** repeats mistakes you already paid for.

## The steps

1. **Recall.** Pull everything you already know from your second brain: notes, past decisions, prior research, contacts. Cheap retrieval first.
2. **Gap.** State plainly what you do not know yet, the open questions.
3. **Deep Research.** Answer the gap from the outside world. This can be handed to an external research tool; the framework produces the prompt and ingests the result.
4. **Synthesis.** Merge recall + research into a short **Decision Memo**: problem, what we know, options, risks, recommendation. Only then act.

## Why a named loop

Named loops (OODA, Build-Measure-Learn, PDCA) become real methods when they are backed by tooling and habit, not just marketing. RDR is backed by:

- a recall engine (semantic + associative),
- a deep-research handoff format,
- a Decision Memo template,
- versioning that maps product progress to RDR cycles.

## Levels

- **L0 Quick:** a question or small fix. Plain recall.
- **L1 Recall:** a new module or hypothesis. Recall thoroughly, do not decide yet.
- **L2 Recall + Deep Research:** anything strategic (new product, market, architecture). Run the full loop, end with a Decision Memo.

## Anti-gimmick rule

RDR must change what you do, not just how you describe it. If a "loop" step adds no decision value, drop it.
